Tetris Gameplay

Origin of Tetris

Tetris was made by Russian computer engineer Alexey Pajitnov in 1984. He was working at the Dorodnitsyn Computing Centre in the Soviet Union. Pajitnov created the game as a fun puzzle because he loved a game called pentominoes.

Tetris Variations

Tetris has many variations since it first came out. It started with classic 8-bit versions on old consoles and has evolved to include modern multiplayer versions on mobile devices. Here are some of its different versions:

  • Tetris Blitz - A fast-paced, time-limited version with power-ups.
  • Tetris 99 - A competitive battle royale version where players compete against 98 others.
  • Tetris: The Grand Master - Known for its difficulty and competitive arcade gameplay.
  • Classic Tetris - The original version, especially played by high-score enthusiasts in tournaments and streaming events.
  • Tetris Mobile - A popular mobile version that offers various modes, from marathon to multiplayer.
  • Puyo Puyo Tetris - A mix of Tetris and Puyo Puyo puzzle mechanics.
  • Tetris Effect - Combines visuals and music to create an immersive experience.

Impact on Gaming

Tetris revolutionized the puzzle genre and is considered one of the greatest video games of all time. Its influence can be seen in modern games that incorporate block mechanics and puzzle-solving challenges. Tetris has sold over 100 million copies worldwide and continues to be a beloved classic.

Tournaments and Competitive Scene

The Tetris competitive scene has grown over the years, with the annual Classic Tetris World Championship drawing top players from around the world. Speedrunning and high-score competitions are major aspects of Tetris fandom, with world records constantly being broken.

Technology and Platforms

Tetris started on the Electronika 60 but soon appeared on other systems like the IBM PC, NES, and Game Boy. It has since been on almost every platform you can think of, from arcades to mobile phones. Tetris remains one of the most widely available games ever made.
